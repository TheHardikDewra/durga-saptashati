Sri Durga Saptashati (also called Devi Mahatmyam or Chandi Path) is the most revered text of the Shakta tradition - 700 verses across 13 chapters from the Markandeya Purana. It tells of the Divine Mother slaying the demons Madhu-Kaitabha, Mahishasura, and Shumbha-Nishumbha in three episodes (charitas), presided over by Mahakali, Mahalakshmi, and Mahasaraswati. Traditionally chanted during Navaratri, daily sadhana, and at moments when the devotee turns to the Mother for refuge.

Why Durga Saptashati Is Chanted

The Saptashati is not a story about Devi - it is Devi. Each verse is a mantra; the entire text is treated as a single mahamantra. Tradition holds that one full, single-pointed recitation is equivalent to a great yajna.

  • Removes obstacles, debt, illness, and fear of enemies
  • Bestows the grace of all three forms of Devi - Mahakali, Mahalakshmi, Mahasaraswati
  • The only text in the Hindu corpus where the Mother declares herself the Supreme
  • Central to Navaratri, Durga Puja, Chandi Homa, and daily Shakta sadhana
  • Contains four supreme hymns: Brahma-Stuti, Shakradi-Stuti, Ya Devi Sarva Bhuteshu, and Narayani Stuti

The Three Charitas

The Saptashati is divided into three episodes (charitas), each presided over by one of the three supreme forms of Devi - corresponding to the three gunas (tamas, rajas, sattva) and the three cosmic functions (destruction of ignorance, sustenance of action, creation of knowledge).

What is Durga Saptashati

Sri Durga Saptashati (श्री दुर्गा सप्तशती) - also called Devi Mahatmyam (देवीमाहात्म्यम्) and Chandi Path - is the heart of Shakta scripture. It appears as chapters 81-93 of the Markandeya Purana. Composed between the 4th and 6th centuries CE, it is the first text in the Hindu canon where the Goddess is declared as the supreme reality - not consort, not aspect, but the One from whom all forms emerge.

  • Source: Markandeya Purana, chapters 81-93
  • Total chapters: 13 (arranged as 1 + 3 + 9)
  • Total mantras: 700 (518 full verses + half/khanda/uvacha mantras)
  • Three episodes: Prathama (Mahakali), Madhyama (Mahalakshmi), Uttama (Mahasaraswati)
  • Frame story: Sage Markandeya tells it to Savarni Manu; within that, sage Medhas teaches it to King Suratha and the merchant Samadhi

The 700 Mantras

The text is called Saptashati - "containing seven hundreds" - because it has 700 mantras. But these are not all complete verses. The breakdown:

537 Sloka mantras (full verses)
38 Ardha-sloka mantras (half verses)
66 Khanda mantras (parts of verses)
57 Uvacha mantras (narrator markers)
2 Punarukta mantras (repetitions)
700 Total mantras

Each is recited as a complete mantra in its own right. The entire text is also treated as a single great mantra - the reason Saptashati paath is considered one of the most efficacious sadhanas in the Shakta tradition.

Trayanga vs Navangam

Two traditional methods for the auxiliary texts (angas) chanted before the main 13 chapters:

Trayanga - 3 angas

The minimum traditional preparation. Recite three stotrams in this order:

  1. Argala Stotram (27 verses) - removes obstacles
  2. Keelaka Stotram (16 verses) - unlocks the mantras
  3. Devi Kavacham (61 verses) - protective armor

Followed by the Navarna mantra, then the chapters.

Navangam - 9 angas

The complete preparation including:

  1. Devi Nyasa - placement of Devi in the body
  2. Devi Avahana - invocation
  3. Devi Namani - the names
  4. Argala Stotram
  5. Keelaka Stotram
  6. Devi Hrudaya - heart of Devi
  7. Dhala - shield
  8. Devi Dhyana - meditation verse
  9. Devi Kavacham

The Saptamatrika - 7 Mothers

In Chapter 8, when Raktabija enters the battle, the Saptamatrika emerge from the gods' energies to assist Devi. Each is the shakti of a major god - drawing his weapon, vahana, and cosmic role into a feminine form. Together they represent Devi's organising principle: every divine power has its shakti.

The Frame Story

The Saptashati is told within a frame:

King Suratha, dispossessed by his ministers, and merchant Samadhi, abandoned by his wife and sons, meet in the forest hermitage of Sage Medhas. Both are bewildered: why does attachment still bind them to those who betrayed them? Medhas explains that this is the work of Mahamaya - and proceeds to narrate the entire Devi Mahatmyam to teach them the supremacy of the Goddess.

This story sits within a larger frame: Sage Markandeya tells it to Krushtuki, who tells it to Savarni Manu (the eighth Manu of this kalpa). At the end, Suratha receives the boon of becoming Savarni Manu in his next life, and Samadhi receives the boon of liberation through self-knowledge.

How to Chant - Etiquette

  • Bathe and wear clean clothes before chanting. Sit on a clean asana facing east or north.
  • Begin with a short prayer to Ganesha and your Guru. End with Aparadha Kshamapana and Om Shanti.
  • Maintain ekagrata (single-pointed focus). The Saptashati's power is proportional to the chanter's concentration.
  • Never interrupt the Madhyama Charita (chapters 2-4) mid-recitation. If interrupted, restart it.
  • The Navarna mantra is traditionally chanted at the junction of each chapter (chapter-sandhi).
  • If the full paath is not possible, even the Saptashloki Durga or Siddha Kunjika alone confer the merit of the full text.
  • Women may chant Durga Saptashati at any time. Some traditions advise mental recitation during menstruation; chanting is never barred.
